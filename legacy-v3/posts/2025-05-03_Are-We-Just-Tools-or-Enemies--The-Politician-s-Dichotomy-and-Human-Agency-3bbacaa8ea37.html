Are We Just Tools or Enemies? The Politician’s Dichotomy and Human Agency

“Politicians divide mankind into two classes: tools and enemies.” 
— Friedrich Nietzsche

Nietzsche, the 19th-century philosopher who dissected power like a surgeon, saw through the games of those who rule. His words feel eerily prescient today, where politics often reduces people to mere instruments or threats. Are we just pawns in their power plays? Or can we reclaim our humanity?

This isn’t just abstract philosophy — it’s unfolding in real time. From nationalist demagogues to corporate-backed leaders, the division of humanity into useful tools and dangerous enemies is a playbook as old as civilisation. But must we accept this fate?

1. The Philosophy of Division: How Power Splits Humanity

Nietzsche wasn’t alone in seeing this divide. Philosophers across time have warned of how those in power categorise people to maintain control.

  • Karl Marx saw workers as tools of capitalism — exploited for labor, discarded when useless.
  • Michel Foucault argued that power “divides to conquer” — creating hierarchies (rich/poor, citizen/foreigner) to justify control.
  • Hannah Arendt, in The Origins of Totalitarianism, showed how regimes dehumanise enemies to justify oppression.

Metaphor: Politics operates like a factory — some are cogs in the machine (tools), while others are labeled as defects to be removed (enemies).

Question: When did you last feel like a tool? When were you made to see someone as an enemy?

2. The Political Framing of Humanity: Geography and Mindset

Politicians don’t just govern — they define reality for millions. They redraw borders, rewrite history, and reshape identities to fit their agendas.

A. Geographical Division: Lines in the Sand

  • Colonialism: Europeans carved Africa into artificial nations, pitting tribes against each other (“divide and rule”).
  • Modern Nationalism: Leaders like Vladimir Putin frame Ukraine as an “enemy” to justify war, while Russians are “tools” of patriotism.
  • Border Politics: Walls (U.S.-Mexico, Israel-Palestine) turn migrants into enemies and citizens into tools of border enforcement.

B. Mindset Division: The Battle for Belief

  • Propaganda: The Nazi regime turned Jews into enemies and ordinary Germans into tools of genocide.
  • Partisan Polarisation: Fox News vs. MSNBC — each side paints the other as existential threats, turning voters into loyalist tools.
  • Social Media Algorithms: They profit by inflaming divisions — making us tools of engagement and enemies in comment wars.

Quote: “The most effective way to destroy people is to deny and obliterate their own understanding of their history.” — George Orwell

3. When Politicians Worsen the Social Climate: Case Studies in Division

Some leaders don’t just exploit divisions — they weaponise them.

A. Hitler’s Germany: The Ultimate Enemy-Making Machine

  • Tools: Ordinary Germans were mobilized as soldiers, workers, and believers in the Reich.
  • Enemies: Jews, Roma, LGBTQ+, and dissidents were labeled as vermin — dehumanised to justify extermination.
  • Tactic: The Big Lie — repeat a falsehood enough, and even the absurd becomes truth.

B. Netanyahu’s Israel: Perpetual War as a Political Strategy

  • Tools: Israeli citizens are told they must support endless military action for survival.
  • Enemies: Palestinians are framed as terrorists, their humanity erased in political discourse.
  • Result: A cycle of violence that keeps hardliners in power.

C. Trump’s America: “Us vs. Them” as a Campaign Strategy

  • Tools: The “MAGA” base, used for votes and rallies.
  • Enemies: Immigrants (“rapists”), liberals (“traitors”), media (“the enemy of the people”).
  • Legacy: A country more fractured than ever.

Metaphor: Like arsonists posing as firefighters, these leaders create crises to “solve” them — keeping people dependent on their rule.

4. The Human Dilemma: Complicit or Resistant?

We aren’t just victims — we’re often participants.

  • The Banality of Compliance: (Hannah Arendt) Many Nazis were ordinary people following orders. Today, how many of us go along with divisive politics out of convenience?
  • The Nihilism Trap: “Why bother? They’re all corrupt.” — This apathy only helps those in power.
  • Consumer-Citizens: We’re tools of the economy — valued only for our spending, not our voices.

Question: When have you been silent because it was easier?

5. Resisting the Dichotomy: What Are Humans to Do?

We must refuse to be reduced.

A. Recognize the Manipulation

  • When a politician calls a group “thugs,” ask: Who benefits from this framing?
  • When you’re told “just focus on your work,” ask: Am I being turned into a silent tool?

B. Solidarity Over Division

  • Labor Movements: Workers unite across race, gender, and nationality (e.g., Amazon unions).
  • Grassroots Activism: Climate strikers, BLM, and anti-war protests reject enemy narratives.

C. Reclaim Agency

  • Philosophy: Like Camus’ “rebel,” we must defy absurd divisions.
  • Action: Vote critically. Demand transparency. Support independent media.

“The only way to deal with an unfree world is to become so absolutely free that your very existence is an act of rebellion.” — Albert Camus

Final Thought

Nietzsche’s warning was a challenge, not a verdict. Politicians will always try to split us into tools and enemies — but humanity’s task is to refuse the division.

We are neither.
We are more.

Call to Action: Next time you hear a divisive narrative — pause. Ask who gains. Then choose solidarity.
