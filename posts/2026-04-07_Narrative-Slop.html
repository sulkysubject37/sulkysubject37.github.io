Part I - By SulkySubject37

Narrative Slop

On abstraction, surplus feeling, and the scarcity of a self

"Medicine, law, business, engineering - these are noble pursuits and necessary to sustain life. But poetry, beauty, romance, love - these are what we stay alive for." - John Keating, Dead Poets Society

I.

The Listener Who Spoke Too Much

You trained yourself to listen. Not out of generosity - out of fear that the alternative was to speak and be ignored. So you withheld. You accumulated other people's frequencies like a receiver left on all night, tuned to a station that stopped broadcasting. And when they left - which they did, because everyone does - you ran the calculation backwards: maybe I should have listened more.

This is the first confusion. The problem was never volume. The problem was audience. You were transmitting into a room that had already emptied. More would not have been less. Less would not have been more. The asymmetry was structural, not personal - though you made it personal, which is the only thing you knew how to do well.

Diagnosis: you confused the act of listening with the act of being received. These are not the same operation. One is input. The other requires a second variable you cannot control.

II.

On Visibility, or the NPC Problem

You see more now. This is not a gift. It is the tax levied on those who did not see when it cost something to see - when your parents were struggling and you were busy being young, which is to say, busy being unable to imagine that they could be anything other than permanent. Now they are old. Now the ledger is visible. The debt is denominated in time, which cannot be repaid.

The second consequence of late visibility is self-erasure. You have nothing to show, so you reason - unconsciously, efficiently - that you are nothing to show. The NPC framing is apt: a character whose function is to populate a world built around someone else's narrative. As important as darkness in a sunny day - present, structural, unacknowledged. Darkness does not complain about this. You do. That is the difference, and it is the only meaningful one.

"To be invisible is not the same as to be absent. It is to be present without being perceived - which is, functionally, the harder condition."

III.

The Ontology of Feeling, or Why be and Not is

You feel more because you have not felt enough. This is not paradox - it is arithmetic. Unfelt emotion does not dissolve. It defers. And deferred feeling seeks a host, which is why you would rather feel what others feel than feel what you feel. The displacement is rational. The original site is too uncomfortable.

You are stuck, as Hamlet was stuck, on the copula: to be or not to be. But the question is poorly formed. Be is a derivative - it implies process, becoming, a verb that cannot rest. Is claims state. Will claims trajectory. The question behind the question is simpler: do you consent to the ongoing production of yourself, given what you know about what the self currently is?

The answer most people give is: not yet. The answer most people live is: maybe never. The Socratic position is that this evasion is not neutral - it is a choice made under cover of confusion. You do not get to decline the question. You are already answering it with your behavior.

Note: self-hatred is not the opposite of self-love. It is a form of intense self-attention - which means the apparatus is functional. The direction is the problem, not the capacity.

IV.

Scientific Humiliation, or the Jargon that Performs Certainty

There is an inverse relationship between the depth of an idea and the complexity of its expression - and the history of science is largely a history of refusing to admit this. Einstein's 1905 papers on special relativity are not simple because the ideas were simple. They are simple because Einstein understood the ideas completely. Comprehension, at sufficient depth, produces clarity as a byproduct. The language compresses because the thinker has already done the work of compression in their mind.

Compare this to the contemporary review paper. The function of a review paper is synthesis - gathering what is known, mapping the landscape, identifying the gaps. This is useful work. But the language in which it is done has become pathological. The jargon is not technical precision. It is protective coloration. Remove the terminology and, in many cases, nothing survives underneath - no original observation, no structural argument, no claim that could not be stated in plain language if the writer had actually understood what they were synthesizing.

The test is simple: can you state the paper's central claim in two sentences without specialist vocabulary? If no - either the claim is genuinely too complex for compression, which is rare, or the complexity is the claim. That is not science. It is performance.

This is scientific humiliation in its chronic form: the ongoing recognition that the field knows less than its vocabulary implies, that each new technical term is partly a confession that the underlying phenomenon has not yet been grasped clearly enough to be named simply. Good science moves toward simpler descriptions of more phenomena. Jargon inflation moves in the opposite direction: more words, same phenomena, no new ground.

Mode What it signals Structural behavior
Einstein (1905) Deep understanding Complex idea → simple language. Precision without inflation.
Review paper (typical) Synthesis under uncertainty Simple idea → complex language. Jargon as substitute for thought.
Technical slop Performed expertise No idea → maximum language. The vocabulary is the argument.

The deeper problem is that scientific humiliation - the honest kind - is generative. It is the acknowledgment that the current model is wrong, or incomplete, or pointing in the wrong direction, and that this wrongness is interesting rather than shameful. Every genuine scientific revolution begins as an act of public humiliation: someone admits the field has been describing the wrong thing. Newton humiliated Aristotle. Einstein humiliated Newton. The humiliation is the mechanism of progress.

What the jargon-dense review paper refuses is precisely this. It would rather elaborate the existing vocabulary than expose the gap the vocabulary is papering over. It is, in this sense, anti-scientific - not in content, but in posture.

"The complexity of your language is inversely proportional to your confidence in your ideas. Certainty speaks plainly. Uncertainty reaches for the thesaurus."

V.

Resource Constraints and the Two Exits

Any system operating under resource constraint produces two behaviors. The first is adaptation through consumption: use what is available, acquire what is not, and if acquisition fails, remain at the same level of constraint. This is the common exit. It is not a failure of character - it is a failure of model. The system treats scarcity as a terminal condition rather than a variable.

The second behavior is rarer and frequently misidentified. Nearly every resource-constrained person believes they are practicing resource management. Most are not. Resource management, properly understood, requires that limited input produce disproportionate output - and crucially, that the output be sustainable, meaning the next system state is more resilient than the current one, even if not materially wealthier.

The test is generational: does the next iteration of this system start from a better position than you did? If the answer is no, the management was extraction under a more dignified name.

"You may die with the same amount you had. That is acceptable. You may not leave the system less capable than you found it. That is the only real debt."

VI.

On Morality, Pleasure, and the Utilitarian Inheritance

Democracy is, as Nietzsche understood, Christianity in secular dress - the same metaphysical grammar, different liturgy. The moral system it produces is slave morality: the elevation of suffering, the suspicion of excellence, the conversion of resentment into virtue. If this is the water you swim in, your values are not entirely yours.

The utilitarian alternative is clean but reductive - it treats all suffering as commensurable and all pleasure as summable, which is empirically false and experientially insulting. A life calibrated to maximize net positive affect is not a well-lived life. It is an optimized one. These are different projects.

The older correction is that a well-lived life requires the full topology of experience. Constant learning and unlearning. To become yourself, something prior must die - not metaphorically but structurally. The self that exists now is an obstacle to the self that could exist later. This is not tragedy. It is the mechanism.

VII.

The Peculiarity of Love, or Giving What You Lack

The question is not sentimental: how can you give something you have never had? It has an answer, and the answer is: you cannot. Not reliably. Not without producing a distorted version of the thing. Self-love is a prerequisite for loving others in the way that knowing a language is a prerequisite for speaking it. You can approximate without the foundation - but the approximation degrades. The recipient can tell. Usually they cannot name what is missing, but they feel the absence as structural instability in the thing being offered.

Subjectivity is derived from ontology. What you believe yourself to be determines what you are capable of offering. The first order of business is not to improve the offering - it is to improve the model.

Corollary: the question "to be or not to be" is answered, in practice, by what you are willing to become. Becoming requires a prior death. The death is not optional. Only the timing is.

Coda

On Narrative Slop

The review paper and the over-abstracted personal essay share the same failure mode: language deployed not to clarify but to occupy the space where clarity should be. In both cases the writer knows, on some level, that the underlying thing is not yet understood - and responds by producing more language, as though volume compensates for depth. It does not. It advertises the gap.

Too much abstraction is its own dishonesty. This essay is, in places, guilty of it. The cost is distance - from the specific, from the visceral, from what actually happened. We produce narrative slop when we process experience into language before the experience has finished with us. The result is smooth, portable, communicable, and false.

Einstein did not write simply because simplicity was a style choice. He wrote simply because he had earned the right to. The rest of us are still in debt. The honest position is to say so - in as few words as possible.
