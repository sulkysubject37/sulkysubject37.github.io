Introduction: The Time-Traveling Sound Architects

Peter Cat Recording Co. (PCRC) doesn’t just make music — they resurrect eras. Imagine a smoky 1970s Kolkata nightclub colliding with a Delhi garage, filtered through Brooklyn’s indie grit. Their sound is a labyrinth of jazz, psychedelia, and Bollywood nostalgia, defying algorithms and genres. This isn’t a band profile — it’s a backstage pass to their analog universe.

The Band: Architects of a Sonic Cult

  1. Suryakant Sawhney (Vocals, Guitar, Synth)
  • The Mad Poet: Channels Leonard Cohen’s brooding depth and Kishore Kumar’s playfulness.
  • Secret Weapon: A modified 1970s sitar-guitar hybrid, heard in “Memory Box.”

2. Karan Singh (Drums)

  • The Rhythm Alchemist: Merges Max Roach’s jazz improvisation with punk’s raw energy.
  • Key Track: “Portrait of a Time” for its hypnotic, cascading beats.

3. Rohit Gupta (Keys, Trumpet)

  • The New Orchestral Voice: Brings jazz-trained brass textures to PCRC.

4. Dhruv Bhola (Bass)

  • The Groove Cartographer: Blends Motown basslines with Bengali folk cadences.

5. Kartik Pillai (Synth, Guitar)

  • The Analog Sorcerer: Crafts textures using a Soviet-era Polivoks-M synth (also used in Stranger Things).

Inspirations: The Ghosts in Their Machines

PCRC’s sound is a séance of music legends:

  • R.D. Burman: Tabla rhythms in “Porcelain” mirror “Dum Maro Dum.”
  • Serge Gainsbourg: Suryakant’s smoky vocals echo “Je t’aime… moi non plus.”
  • The Velvet Underground: Raw, poetic dissonance meets synth experimentation.
  • A.R. Rahman: Genre collisions akin to “Rangeela’s” audacity.

Untold Stories: PCRC’s Hidden DNA

1. The Garage That Birthed a Cult

  • Secret Studio: Their earliest demos (2010–2012) were recorded in a crumbling Delhi garage owned by Suryakant’s grandmother. No AC, just a rusty fan and a moth-eaten Persian rug. “The heat forced us into slower, jazzier tempos,” says Kartik.
  • Name Origin: “Peter Cat” pays homage to a defunct 1970s Kolkata nightclub where Suryakant’s parents first met.

2. The Lost Album: Monsoon Electric

  • A 2015 synth-heavy album deemed “too dystopian” was scrapped. Only two tracks survived: “Bismillah” and “Ghosts Who Answer Back.”
  • Era-Defining Gear: The band’s sitar-guitar hybrid was born here, crafted from a broken Yamaha PS-20.

3. Bollywood’s Hidden Hand

  • Sampled Ghosts: The synth arpeggio in “Porcelain” samples Lata Mangeshkar’s laugh from the 1972 film Parichay.
  • Forbidden Track: “Taxi Driver in Bombay” interpolates R.D. Burman’s “Dum Maro Dum” without clearance — played only live.

4. Brooklyn Survival Jobs

During their 2018 NYC residency:

  • Suryakant washed dishes at a vegan café, scribbling lyrics on napkins.
  • Dhruv busked in subway stations, testing PCRC demos on commuters.
  • Inspiration Strikes: “Floated By” was written after Suryakant found a discarded love letter on the L train.

5. Analog Obsessions

  • No Digital Allowed: Bismillah was recorded on a Tascam 388 tape machine — zero edits.
  • Soviet Synth: Kartik’s Polivoks-M adds lo-fi grit, sourced from a Kyiv flea market.

Exclusive Rituals: How PCRC Summons Sound

  1. Pre-Show Elixir: Hot Limca soda + black pepper — a Delhi street vendor’s recipe for “vocal clarity.”
  2. Lyric Craft: Suryakant types lyrics on a 1970s Hindi typewriter for retro authenticity.
  3. Studio Rule: Every take is recorded twice — once sober, once after a shot of Old Monk rum.

“Bring the Class Down” — PCRC’s Anti-Elitist Manifesto (might be an exaggeration)

Quote Context:
In a 2011 interview with LFTC, former band member Rohan Kulshreshtha revealed PCRC’s unspoken motto:

“The motto if the band is to ‘bring the class down.’ We’re here to melt genres, mock pretension, and make music that feels alive, not airbrushed.”

What “Bring the Class Down” Means

  1. Genre Sabotage:
  • PCRC’s sound mocks the idea of “highbrow” music. A jazz trumpet solo might crash into a Bollywood synth riff (e.g., “Floated By”), dismantling cultural hierarchies.
  • Python Parallel: Their music is like a machine learning model trained on chaotic data — unpredictable, raw, and human.

2. Analog Over Digital:

  • They use Soviet synths and tape machines to reject the “polish” of modern DAWs.
  • “Hiss and crackle are our band members too,” Rohan joked in a 2020 podcast.

3. Lyric Democracy:
Suryakant’s lyrics blend Urdu poetry (“Bismillah”) with Delhi street slang (“Clown on the 22nd Dance Floor”), refusing to cater to elitist sensibilities.

Why PCRC Matters Now?

In an age of algorithm-driven playlists, PCRC is a handcrafted rebellion. Their music is a palimpsest of Delhi’s chaos, analog ghosts, and borrowed Bollywood sighs — a manifesto for artistic freedom.
